Covid-19: An Interactive Story

Social Distance. Lockdown. Flatten the Curve.
You've heard it all, but what does it really mean?
Why should you care?
This DIY story explains what these things are and how they are supposed to help us combat the Coronavirus.

This story uses simple simulations of a Coronavirus outbreak in the real world.
You can interact with these simulations to try out the effects of different measures.
A box with bouncing balls represents a community of people, such as a city.

Initially some people are infected with Coronavirus.
The virus can spread throughout the community when people come in to contact with each other.
You can see by the colour of a ball what state the person is in:
  • Healthy
  • Sick
  • Recovered
  • Dead
Healthy people can get sick when they meet another sick person.

If they are careful, they can reduce the risk of infection: they could wear a mask, wash their hands after the meeting, or keep their distance.

The risk of getting infected always increases when the meeting lasts longer.
After a certain amount of time, sick people hopefully recover.

But unfortunately not everyone does: some sick people die.

We have to act to avoid this as much as possible.
This simulation shows the effects of following hygiene recommendations.

Better hygiene can limit the spread of the virus. Better hygiene includes:
  • Washing hands regularly
  • Sneezing and coughing into the elbow
  • No touching of the face
  • Wearing fase masks

Try out different levels of hygiene to see what changes.
You may have noticed that better hygiene levels "flatten the curve" - this means that the number of infections increases more slowly.
Good hygiene alone will not prevent the majority of a population from becoming infected though. A population that relies only on good hygiene may overload their health service.
As more patients are hospitalised, the health service is put under greater strain.

Eventually the health service may run out of supplies, such as ventilators or face masks.

They may even run out of space in intensive care units (ICUs).
This will lead to an increased chance of death for those hospitalised with Coronavirus, as well as those without Coronavirus who may not receive life-saving care.
In countries with less developed health services, the health service will be overloaded sooner.

Try running the simulations with different amounts of ICUs to see how easily a health service can be stretched beyond capacity.
One of the most effective ways to flatten the curve is by self-quarantining - leaving the house only when necessary.

Leaving the house only when necessary limits your risk of becoming infected and the virus spreading to the whole population.
Quarantining large parts of the population can help to root out the virus, but can also make it difficult for society to function.
High levels of testing can identify individuals with Coronavirus earlier and advise them to quarantine before they spread the virus to others.
In this final simulation you can try out any combination of measures. We encourage you to develop your understanding by playing around with the simulation.
